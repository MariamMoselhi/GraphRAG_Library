--- Page 3 ---
Importance of mobile devices
• Business is increasingly conducted on mobile devices.
• Organizations offer mobile applications to serve their customers.
• While mobile applications are convenient, they introduce significant
security risks when these applications are not continuously monitored
for new vulnerabilities and potential threats.

--- Page 4 ---
Significance of mobile threats
Example: My Fitness Pal data breach incident
• About 150 million users have been affected by the Under Armour’s
health and fitness tracking app “My Fitness Pal” data breach.
• Unauthorized party gained information like usernames and email
addresses for customers.
• Public shares fell nearly 5% just days after the breach was
announced.

--- Page 5 ---
Mobile Application Attacks
• Device level attacks aim at mobile devices like smartphones and tablets,
seeking to either access the network of the device or retrieve sensitive data
saved on it.
• Vulnerable binaries may be analyzed and reverse-engineered to produce
duplicates or alter the original application's source code, undermining its
integrity.
• Insecure data storage is based on the belief that storing data on the client side
is safe, yet attackers can employ tools to retrieve important information from
these storage areas.

--- Page 6 ---
Mobile Application Attacks (Cont.)
• Rooted Android or jailbroken iOS devices are susceptible to exploitation,
granting attackers deeper access to the device’s system or enabling the
installation of malicious apps.

--- Page 7 ---
Mobile-Specific Attack Vectors
Mobile devices have some special features that results in new attacks that
are different from attacks on computers.
• Text Messages:
• SMS messages is being employed by hackers to replace email messages as a
means for their social engineering attacks.
• Spam and phishing SMS messages are not usually filtered out as much as emails.
So, SMS messages are ideal for spam and phishing attacks.
• Users should be cautious about clicking a random link in an SMS text just as
random links received by email.
• Near Field Communication:
• NFC could be used to attack a mobile device by beaming malicious payload to a
vulnerable application on the device.
• Users should be aware of which NFC tags their device responds to.

--- Page 8 ---
Mobile Attack Vectors (Cont.)
• QR Codes:
• QR codes could embed URLs, send data to an application on a mobile device, and
so on.
• QR codes could also be used to open malicious pages.
• GPS:
• Unencrypted location data could be exploited by attackers to acquire the location
and GPS data off mobile users.
• User’s location could present a larger risk of sophisticated social engineering
attacks.

Common Mobile App
Vulnerabilities
Mobile applications could be prone to high severity vulnerabilities such as:
• Broken SSL
• Cert Validation Insecure
• Data leakage
• Privilege abuse
• Unencrypted Device ID, Location Data, Credentials, Personally Identifiable
Information (PII), …etc.
• Credential theft
• Vulnerable binaries.
• The use of rooted Android or jailbroken iOS devices

Cybersecurity for mobile devices
Cybersecurity for mobile devices presents unique challenges compared to
traditional computers.
• Portability and Accessibility:
• Increased Risk: Mobile devices are constantly on the move, making them more
susceptible to theft, loss, and exposure to different networks.
• Remote Access: It is more common on mobile devices to remotely access
sensitive data and applications. This increases the potential for unauthorized
access if security measures are weak.
• Diverse Operating Systems and App Stores:
• Fragmentation: Mobile devices run on different operating systems (Android, iOS,
…etc.), each OS has its own vulnerabilities and update cycles.
• App Store Security: Applications downloaded from third-party app stores could
introduce risks.

--- Page 11 ---
Cybersecurity for mobile devices
(Cont.)
• User Behavior and Awareness:
• Less Cautious Usage: Mobile users often have less security awareness compared
to desktop users, leading to careless habits such as selecting weak passwords,
downloading from untrusted sources, and clicking on suspicious links.
• Social Engineering Targets: Mobile devices are ideal targets for social
engineering attacks, such as phishing scams and smishing (SMS phishing).
• Hardware and Software Limitations:
• Processing Power: Mobile devices have less processing power and memory
compared to desktop and laptop computers. This limits the effectiveness of
certain security solutions.
• Battery Life: Security features like encryption and frequent scanning can drain
battery faster which impacts user experience.

--- Page 12 ---
Cybersecurity for mobile devices
(Cont.)
• Evolving Threat Landscape:
• Mobile-specific Threats: Cybercriminals are increasingly targeting mobile devices
with malware, spyware, and other malicious attacks.
• Rapid Development: The mobile environment is constantly progressing, with
new devices, apps, and vulnerabilities emerging regularly.

Addressing mobile cybersecurity
challenges
To address mobile cybersecurity challenges, mobile cybersecurity requires
a defense-in-depth approach, including:
1. Strong Passwords and Biometric Authentication:
• Use strong unique passwords.
• Enable biometric authentication (fingerprint, facial recognition).
2. Regular Software Updates:
• Keep your device’s OS and apps updated to the latest versions to patch
vulnerabilities.
3. Secure Wi-Fi Connections:
• Avoid public Wi-Fi networks for sensitive activities and use a VPN to encrypt your
connection.

--- Page 14 ---
Addressing mobile cybersecurity

4. Mobile Security Software: Consider using a well-known mobile
security software to protect against malware, phishing, and other
threats.
5. App Permissions: Avoid excessive permissions which can compromise
your privacy and security. (A Calculator app doesn’t need to access
your webcam!)
6. Data Backups: Regularly back up your device’s data to protect against
loss, theft, or ransomware attacks.
7. User Awareness and Training: Stay informed about the latest mobile
security threats and best practices. Mobile Security Software:
Consider using reputable mobile security software to protect
against malware, phishing, and other threats.

How businesses could mitigate
mobile risks
1. Mobile App Security: Companies ought to emphasize the security of their
mobile applications by performing thorough vulnerability assessments,
carrying out consistent code reviews, and employing strong data encryption
techniques to effectively protect against possible threats.
2.
Authentication: Robust authentication techniques must be put in place to
guarantee that only approved individuals have access to mobile applications.
Implementing strict password policies is crucial in enhancing the security of
the app.
3.
Employee Training: Providing employee training is crucial for fostering a
workforce that prioritizes security. These programs teach staff about mobile
security best practices, equipping them with the knowledge to identify and
effectively react to potential threats

--- Page 16 ---
How businesses could mitigate
mobile risks (Cont.)
4. Network Security: It is essential to promote the use of secure and
reliable Wi-Fi networks among employees for work-related tasks. This
helps reduce the dangers linked to connecting to unprotected
networks, which are frequently targeted by cybercriminals.
5. Device Security: The implementation of Mobile Device Management
(MDM) or Enterprise Mobility Management (EMM) solutions is
essential. These solutions assist in securing the mobile devices utilized
by staff, making certain that they comply with established security
policies and configurations.
6. Compliance: To prevent legal and regulatory issues, companies need
to make sure that the development and use of mobile applications
comply with regulations specific to their industry, including adherence
to data protection and privacy laws relevant to the sector.

--- Page 17 ---
How businesses could mitigate
mobile risks (Cont.)
7. Privacy: Reviewing data collection and sharing practices is essential.
Businesses should strive to align these practices with privacy
regulations to protect user data and maintain trust.
8. Monitoring: Consistently observing mobile app activity is essential for
quickly detecting potential security breaches. This preventive strategy
allows companies to respond rapidly should a security issue arise.
9. Incident Response: Creating a comprehensive incident response
strategy is crucial. This strategy must define explicit steps for quickly
managing security breaches, reducing possible harm, and facilitating a
rapid recovery process.
